1. Harkat – مختصر حرکات (الحركات)

Indo‑Pak نام: Zabar (Fatha), Zer (Kasra), Pesh (Damma)

علامت: ـَ, ـِ, ـُ

مثال: بَ، بِ، بُ (ba, bi, bu) – حرف ب پر مختصر حرکات

  • Basic vowel marks in Arabic.
  • Appear above (ـَ/ـُ) or below (ـِ) the letter.
  • Fatha = short “a”, Kasra = short “i”, Damma = short “u”.
  • Marks guide pronunciation, not letters themselves.
  • Applied to consonants only.
  • Essential for correct pronunciation and avoiding meaning errors.

2. Madd – مدّ (المدّ)

Indo‑Pak نام: Madd

علامت: ا, و, ي

مثال: قَالَ، يَقُولُ، قِيلَ (qaala, yaqoolu, qeela) – He said, He says, It was said

  • "Madd" means to stretch the vowel sound.
  • When Alif follows Fatha, Waw follows Damma, or Ya follows Kasra.
  • Madd Asli (natural): 2 counts elongation.
  • Madd Far’i (secondary): depends on other rules; 4–6 counts.
  • Types: Madd Muttasil, Munfasil, Lazim, ‘Arid Lis‑Sukoon.
  • Recognizing Madd helps proper Tajweed rhythm.

3. Leen – نرمی (اللين)

Indo‑Pak نام: Leen

علامت: وْ, يْ after Fatha

مثال: خَوْف، بَيْت (khawf, bayt) – Fear, House

  • Leen means softness in pronunciation.
  • Occurs when Waw or Ya has Sukoon and previous letter has Fatha.
  • Sound flows gently—not stretched like Madd.
  • Enhances fluency and musicality.
  • Distinct from Madd: Madd is stretched, Leen is soft.

4. Sukoon – سُکُون (السكون)

Indo‑Pak نام: Jazm (جزم)

علامت: ْ

مثال: يَذْهَبُ، قُلْ (yadh‑habu, qul) – He goes, Say

  • Sign indicates a letter with no vowel sound.
  • Causes the letter to be read quickly—at rest.
  • Occurs in middle or end of words.
  • Important for Idgham, Ikhfa, Iqlab rules.
  • At end of a verse, can be temporary due to waqf (stopping).

5. Tashdeed – تشدید (التشديد)

Indo‑Pak نام: Tashdeed

علامت: ّ

مثال: رَبِّ، اللَّهُ (rabbi, Allahu) – My Lord, Allah

  • Shaddah means the letter is doubled.
  • Pause briefly, then pronounce the doubled letter with stress.
  • Essential for clarity and correct meaning (e.g. ربِّ ≠ ربی).
  • Used in اللّٰه for emphasis in Divine name.
  • Missing tashdeed changes meanings entirely.

6. Laam – لفظ اللہ کی لام

Indo‑Pak نام: Tafkheem of Laam

علامت: اللّٰه

مثال: اللَّهُ، بِاللَّهِ (Allahu, billahi)

  • Laam is heavy (Tafkhīm) or light (Tarqīq) in Allah’s Name.
  • Heavy if preceded by Fatha or Damma (e.g. اللهَ).
  • Light if preceded by Kasra (e.g. بِاللَّهِ).
  • Beautifies pronunciation of Allah’s Name.

7. Raa – ثقیل/ترقیق الراء (تفخيم وترقيق الراء)

Indo‑Pak نام: Tafkheem/Tarqeeq of Raa

علامت: رَ، رُ، رِ

مثال: رَحْمَٰن، قُرْآن، فِرْعَوْن (Rahmaan, Qur’an, Fir'awn)

  • Raa is read heavy or light depending on surrounding vowels.
  • Heavy (Tafkhīm) with Fatha or Damma.
  • Light (Tarqīq) with Kasra.
  • Deeply affects tone and beauty of recitation.

8. Temporary Madd – المد العارض للسكون

Indo-Pak نام: Aarid Madd (عارض مد)

علامت: ا, ي, و + Sukoon (ْ) on next letter

مثال: الْعَالَمِينَ (Stop) – al-‘aalameen (The Worlds)

  • Occurs when a long vowel is followed by a temporary Sukoon due to Waqf (stopping).
  • This rule only applies when ending the recitation on a word.
  • The vowel is stretched for 2, 4, or 6 counts depending on the style of recitation (Qira'at).
  • This elongation helps beautify the rhythm at the end of verses (Ayaat).
  • One of the most common forms of Madd in actual Quran recitation.
  • Does not apply when the word is connected to the next word in continuous recitation.
  • Reference: Quran Mualim: Madd Arid Lis Sukoon

9. Noon Saakin & Tanween Rules – أحكام النون الساكنة والتنوين

Indo-Pak نام: Noon ke 4 Qaide (نون کے چار قاعدے)

علامت: نْ , ـً ـٍ ـٌ

مثال: مَنْ آمَنَ، أَنْبِئْهُمْ، مِّنْ رَبِّهِمْ (man aamana, anbihum, min rabbihim)

  • These rules apply when Noon Saakin (نْ) or Tanween (ــًــٍــٌ) is followed by another letter.
  • 1. Izhaar (إِظْهَار): Clear pronunciation without nasal sound. Applies when followed by any of the six throat letters: ء، ه، ع، ح، غ، خ.
  • 2. Iqlab (إِقْلَاب): Noon changes to Meem sound (م) with nasalization (Ghunna), but only when followed by ب (Baa).
  • 3. Idghaam (إِدْغَام): Merging of Noon into the next letter. Applies to ي، ر، م، ل، و، ن. Some require Ghunna, some don’t:
    • With Ghunna: ي، ن، م، و
    • Without Ghunna: ل، ر
  • 4. Ikhfaa (إِخْفَاء): Hidden (concealed) Noon with nasal sound. Applies when followed by any of the remaining 15 letters (excluding Izhaar, Iqlab, Idghaam letters).
  • These rules enhance fluency, proper rhythm, and correct Tajweed in recitation.
  • Reference: Riwaq Al Quran Blog

10. Types of Madd – أنواع المدود

Indo-Pak نام: Madd ke Iqsaam (مد کے اقسام)

علامت: ا, و, ي + Hamza (ء) or Sukoon (ْ)

مثال: قَالَ، جَاءَ، الضَّالِّينَ (qaala, jaa’a, ad-daalleen)

  • Madd (مد) means elongation of a vowel sound during recitation.
  • 3 Madd letters: Alif (ا) after Fatha, Waw (و) after Dammah, Ya (ي) after Kasra.
  • Madd Asli / Tabi'y (مد أصلي / طبيعي):
    • Natural Madd.
    • Stretched for 2 counts only.
    • No Hamza (ء) or Sukoon (ْ) follows.
  • Madd Far'ee (مد فرعي): Occurs when the Madd letter is followed by Hamzah or Sukoon.
    • Madd Muttasil (مد متصل): Hamzah comes in the same word. Elongated 4–5 counts. (e.g. جَاءَ)
    • Madd Munfasil (مد منفصل): Hamzah comes in next word. Elongated 2–4 counts. (e.g. فِيٓ أُذُنِهِ)
    • Madd Lazim (مد لازم): Followed by a permanent Sukoon. Must be stretched 6 counts. (e.g. الضَّالِّينَ)
    • Madd ‘Arid Lis-Sukoon (مد عارض للسكون): When the Sukoon occurs due to Waqf (stop). Elongated 2, 4, or 6 counts. (e.g. الْعَالَمِينَ)
    • Madd Badal (مد بدل): When Hamzah is replaced by Madd letter. (e.g. آدَم from أَأْدَم)
    • Madd Leen (مد لين): A soft Madd caused by Waw or Ya with Sukoon after Fatha. (e.g. خَوْف، بَيْت)
  • Knowing types of Madd is essential for correct pronunciation, Tajweed rhythm, and avoiding misinterpretation.
  • Reference: Mishkah Academy: Madd Guide
